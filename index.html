    Go ahead and pick the first example.

    Brain and body weight of cats and dogs

    Context: The scatter plot above shows the brain and body weights of n = 200 cats and n = 200 chihuahuas. The data sets were randomly drawn from a normal distribution whose mean and standard deviations were taken from autopsy findings (Bronson, 1979).

    Setting: Imagine you want to develop (for whatever reason...) a machine learning algorithm that distinguishes cats and chihuahuas from each other, based on the given data. The blue separator represents the result of your algorithm: Everything above the separator is classified as a chihuahua, everything below as a cat.

    Have a look at the sensitivity and specificity values. How do they change when you change the position of the seperator?

    Some things to try
    • Try to set the specificity for cats as high as possible (to 1). How does this affect the sensitivity for cats? Why? (Try it the other way around, too!)
    • Take a look at the contingeny table. Which values in the table lead to changes in specificity and sensitivity? Why?
    • What happens to the specificity and sensitivity for chihuahuas when you adjust the values for cats?
    • Now let's look at the miss rate and false alarm rate (click on the "Settings" button to display the two metrics). How do these two metrics change in relation to sensitivity and specificity?

    Which of the four metrics mentioned (sensitivity, specificity, miss rate, false alarm rate) should you use to answer the following questions?

    • What is the probability that a chihuahua will be wrongly classified as a cat? (answer)
    • What is the probability that a chihuahua will be classified correctly as a chihuahua? (answer)
    • What is the probability that a cat will be classified correctly as a cat? (answer)
    • What is the probability that a cat will be wrongly classified as a "non-cat"? (answer)
    Selection of candidates for a management position

    Context: A key predictor of work performance is the candidate's intelligence. Intelligence and work performance correlate positively with each other (Sackett et al., 2022; Schmidt & Hunter, 1998; Schmitt, 2014).

    The second measure shown in the plot is the applicant's previous leadership experience. It is reasonable to assume that tasks with leadership responsibility will have a positive influence on work performance in future leadership positions and should therefore be taken into account in the application process.

    However, there are a number of systematic differences between women and men when it comes to leadership experience: as women on average take on more care work (e.g. looking after children or relatives) than men, and also take more time off work to do so (OECD, 2014; DeStatis, 2023). Therefore it is reasonable to assume that women (in management positions) have on average less management experience than men of the same age in similar positions.

    Setting: As the HR-Manager responsible for selecting new management staff in a large company, you have access to data on intelligence (assessed via WIT-2; subscale "Reasoning") and and leadership experience (in years) of N = 400 applicants for an open management position. All candidates placed above the separator will be invited to the further application process.

    Your company uses a third-party machine learning model that scores candidates for leadership positions based on predictors such as age, leadership experience and application content. The model calculates a score between 0 and 100, with applicants scoring above 70 being categorised as 'likely to be successful'. The company aims for equal gender representation in management positions.

    (Please keep in mind that this is not real data, and that a real candidate selection process should of course be designed differently!)

    Some things to try
    • Look again at the sensitivity and specificity. How would the values change if the two groups overlapped more (or less)?
    • Is sensitivity or specificity more relevant when it comes to inviting as many likely successful candidates - and as few unlikely successful candidates - as possible?
    • What is a key difference between sensitivity and specificity on the one hand and positive and negative predictive value on the other? How do the calculation and the interpretation of the different metrics differ?
    • Activate the false discovery rate and the false omission rate in the settings. What is their relationship to the positive and negative predictive value?

    Which of the four metrics mentioned (positive predictive value, negative predictive value, false discovery rate, false omission rate) should you use to answer the following questions?

    • Assuming a person receives a positive test result. What is the probability that the person is in fact "likely not successful"? (answer)
    • How likely is it that a candidate with a positive test result is in fact "likely successful"? (answer)
    • How likely is it that a classification as "unlikely successful" is wrong? (answer)
    • What is the probability that a candidate with a negative test result is in fact classified as "unlikely successful"? (answer)
    Depression and anxiety.

    Context: Anxiety and depression are positively correlated. This also leads to a high co-occurrence (comorbidity) of depressive and anxiety syndromes (e.g. Kessler et al., 1999).

    This is also reflected in various questionnaires. For example, the STAI is used to assess trait and state anxiety, and both primary anxious and primary depressed patients score higher in trait anxiety than healthy controls (e.g. Kim et al., 2018; Wilbertz et al., 2010).

    Furthermore, there is evidence suggesting that patients with depression often score higher in trait anxiety than patients with anxiety (possible explanation: STAI may measure unspecific negative affect, rather than specific trait-anxiety; Knowles & Olatunji, 2020; D’Avanzato et al., 2013).

    In addition, patients with anxiety disorders also show increased scores in the BDI-II, a questionnaire on depressive symptoms (e.g. Coles et al., 2001).

    In psychotherapeutic settings it is of elementary importance to diagnose comorbidities in addition to the "primary diagnosis". However, as the treatment of anxiety disorders differs from the treatment of depression, the primary diagnosis has far-reaching implications for treatment, e.g. the problem that is assumed to be central to the persistence of the patients condition should be treated first and be considered the primary diagnosis.

    Setting: As a psychotherapy researcher, you are developing a test to discriminate between patients with depression as primary diagnosis and patients with an anxiety disorder as primary diagnosis.

    (As always, it should be kept in mind that the data shown - although inspired by real world findings - is in fact simulated!)

    Some things to try
    • Let's look at sensitivity and specificity one last time. Assuming you want to make sure that patients with depression who also show high anxiety scores always receive a treatment program for depression (and none for anxiety). Which of the two metrics should you pay attention to? To what extent is there a trade-off here?
    • Activate the positive and neg. likelihood ratio in the settings. Compare both metrics with regard to their interpretation. Which of these metrics should generally be minimized or maximized?
    • What does a positive likelihood ratio of less than 1 indicate? What does a value of exactly 1 indicate?
    • Activate the last four measures (accuracy, F1-Score, diagnostic odds ratio, Youden's J) in the settings. What do they all have in common?
    • What is the key difference between the diagnostic odds ratio on the one hand and accuracy, F1-Score and Youden's J on the other?
    • How does the interpretation of accuracy and diagnostic odds ratio differ from each other?

    Should you use the pos. predictive value, the pos. likelihood ratio, accuracy or the diagnostic odds ratio to answer the following questions?

    • What is the impact of a positive test result on the probability that a patient is diagnosed with depression? (answer)
    • How well does the diagnostic test differentiate between depressive patients and patients with an anxiety disorder in terms of both sensitivity and specificity? (answer)
    • What is the probability that a positive test will actually identify a person with depression and not an anxiety disorder? (answer)
    • What is the proportion of correctly classified depressive patients and patients with an anxiety disorder? (answer)

Tutorial Part 1: Structure

The DiaMetrics user interface consists of four areas:

1) Example-Picker

At the top of the page you can select different data examples that we use in order to illustrate the concepts of sensitivity and specificity (and some other metrics).

We recommend looking at all examples in order, but you can switch between examples at any time.

2) Scatter plot

Below the Example-Picker you will find a scatter plot. There you can see the data of the example you selected with the Example-Picker.

Go ahead and pick an example above. Can you see how the data is displayed in the scatter plot?

The data in each example will be split into two groups. Your goal will be to seperate the two groups using the blue seperator. We'll take a look at that a little later.

3) Metrics & contingency table

Next to the scatter plot you will find some metrics that describe the discrimination of the two groups by the blue separator, including specificity and sensitivity.

Since the discrimination between the two groups is perfect in this example, the values of specificity and sensitivity are 1 (which corresponds to 100%).

Furthermore, you will find a contingency table that shows the number of true and false positives and negatives for both of the groups shown. Only one of these tables is displayed here, namely the one for variable A.

Tutorial Part 2: Seperating groups

Moving the seperator

The (blue) separator can be moved freely using drag-and-drop.

To do this, click and hold one end of the line and drag it to the desired position to change the separation of the groups.

Adjust the separator using drag & drop so that the groups are no longer perfectly separated.
